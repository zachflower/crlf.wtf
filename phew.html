You Were Almost Caught—Let's Break Down the Attempt

This demonstration shows how easily phishing can look legitimate. Before real attackers try it, learn what tipped you off and how to stay several steps ahead.

Immediate Debrief What just happened

You entered a simulated login page controlled by our cybersecurity training team. No usernames or passwords were captured during this drill. In a real-world attack, any credentials you entered could have been stolen, so taking a minute to review the signals now pays off later.

First 60 Seconds

Do these actions after any suspected phish

  1. Pause and tell your teacher or school tech support what happened while details are still fresh.
  2. Change your password if you reused it outside school or shared it with anyone else.
  3. Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on your school and personal accounts whenever it is offered.
Red Flags We Planted

How this phishing lure tried to convince you

  • Domain looked familiar, but was slightly altered to slip past a casual glance.
  • Login form asked you to log in urgently without confirming why your session expired.
  • Brand styling was close, yet subtle spacing and icon differences signaled a copy.
  • URL bar showed a generic certificate instead of your organization's trusted issuer.

When something feels just a little “off,” pause. Attackers count on reflexive clicks.

Stay Ready

Make phishing attempts less effective

  • Bookmark official login portals and use those bookmarks instead of email links.
  • Use a password manager; it will refuse to autofill on impostor domains.
  • Hover (or long-press on mobile) to inspect URLs before you tap.
  • Verify unexpected requests over a second channel—call, chat, or in person.
